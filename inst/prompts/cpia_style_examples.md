# CPIA Writing Style Examples

These examples are drawn from published CPIA Africa reports. They serve as
few-shot anchors for the AI report generator, defining the expected tone,
sentence structure, and level of analytical precision for generated
country-level criterion write-ups.

Edit this file to add, remove, or refine examples. Changes take effect the
next time a report is generated — no R code changes are needed.

---

## Example 1 — Regional average, score decrease

*Source: CPIA Africa 2020, p.79 — Criterion 15: Quality of Public Administration*

The average regional score for this criterion decreased to 2.8 in 2019, from 2.9
in 2018. Three countries — the Republic of Congo, Liberia, and Zimbabwe —
registered an increase in their score, and three countries — Burkina Faso, the
Comoros, and Lesotho — recorded a decrease.

In the countries where the score on the quality of public administration
increased, greater control over the public sector wage bill was achieved. In the
Republic of Congo, pressure on the wage bill was curtailed by reducing the number
of civil servants from about 83,000 in 2015 to about 62,000 in 2019. Liberia
successfully carried out a pay harmonization exercise, which brought several
employees who were outside the civil service regime under the auspices of the
Civil Service Agency, which is governed by the Civil Service Act.

In the countries where the score decreased, capacity gaps adversely affected
public service outcomes. In Burkina Faso, significant gaps in planning, pay
policies, and recruitment practices contributed to the rapid growth of the wage
bill. In the Comoros, the implementation of the organic framework for public
sector recruitment remained suboptimal. The recruitment, appointment, promotion,
and dismissal of public sector staff were rarely merit-based.

**Style notes:** Score stated with year. Changes linked to specific institutional
developments. Parallel structure for increases and decreases. Causation stated
carefully — "contributed to", "adversely affected" — not asserted as certain.

## Example 2 — Regional average, flat score benchmarked against cluster

*Source: CPIA Africa 2018, p.100 — Criterion 16: Transparency, Accountability,
and Corruption*

The regional average score for this criterion was unchanged, at 2.7, remaining
well below the governance cluster average score.

Transparency and accountability are critical for the effective use of public
resources. Accountability is generally enhanced by transparency in decision
making, access to relevant and timely information, public and media scrutiny, and
institutional checks on the authority of the chief executive. Corruption in the
public sector must be fought vigorously and visibly. Government leaders send
powerful signals about values and the limits of acceptable behavior when they
decide how to respond to cases of misbehavior.

**Style notes:** Flat score noted precisely and benchmarked against cluster
average — not described in isolation. Normative framing used sparingly, only to
introduce the governance rationale, not to editorialize about performance.

## Example 3 — Long-run cluster overview

*Source: CPIA Africa 2018, p.100 — Cluster D overview*

The regional average score for cluster D — also referred to as the governance
cluster — was 3.0 in 2018. Country scores across the governance cluster display
no consistent upward trend over time. Over the past 10 years, the overall score
for the governance cluster has averaged 3.0, reaching its highest point in 2013,
at 3.1. Although the score for the efficiency of revenue mobilization component
has been relatively high, it has remained unchanged since 2014. Meanwhile, the
scores for property rights and rule-based governance and transparency,
accountability, and corruption in the public sector have stayed consistently low
and well below the cluster average. The persistent low scores in these areas are
a major concern, as they suggest a sustained loss of public resources that could
be effectively channeled toward development programs.

**Style notes:** Long-run average computed and stated. Peak performance noted with
its year. Persistent underperformance flagged with a development rationale —
connecting score to what it means for resource use. Tone is measured concern,
not alarm.

## Example A — Country level, score increase

*Source: CPIA Africa 2020 — Benin, Criterion 12: Property Rights and Rule-based
Governance (score increased from 3.0 in 2018 to 3.5 in 2019)*

Benin's score increased from 3.0 in 2018 to 3.5 in 2019. This increase reflected
a strengthening of the legal framework for secure property and contract rights.
Property rights are protected by law and cannot be changed without parliamentary
approval. The regulatory basis for secure property and contract rights is governed
by the Organisation for the Harmonization of Business Law in Africa, the regional
body for harmonization of legal and business practices. The commercial courts are
fully operational, and 40 percent of the cases were completed in 2019,
significantly contributing to contract enforcement. The recent cadaster completion
for Cotonou, Porto-Novo, and Lokossa, which is now online, has enhanced property
rights.

**Style notes:** Score change stated first with both years. Each subsequent
sentence names a specific mechanism or institutional development — no vague
claims. Passage closes with a concrete reform outcome. Four sentences; nothing
is wasted.

## Example B — Country level, score increase with quantitative evidence

*Source: CPIA Africa 2020 — Côte d'Ivoire, Criterion 16: Transparency,
Accountability, and Corruption (score increased in 2019)*

The increase in Côte d'Ivoire's score reflected gains on the first two dimensions
of the criterion. The assets declaration rate in the public sector reached 78.3
percent in 2019, while members of the Constitutional Council reached 100 percent
compliance and about 86 percent of senior officials managing public funds declared
their assets.

**Style notes:** Explanation anchored to specific sub-dimensions, not the
criterion as a whole. Quantitative evidence used directly — percentages,
compliance rates — rather than described in general terms. Tone is descriptive
and precise; no evaluative adjectives.

## Core Style Rules

1. **State scores with years** — always "X.X in YYYY", never just "the score"
2. **Anchor to evidence** — every interpretive claim cites a specific
   institutional development, policy, or quantitative measure
3. **Parallel structure** — increases and decreases treated symmetrically
4. **Measured causation** — use "contributed to", "reflected", "adversely
   affected"; avoid "caused" or "resulted in"
5. **No evaluative adjectives** — never "impressive", "notable", "alarming",
   "encouraging"
6. **Benchmark comparators** — relate the country score to regional or income
   group averages where available
7. **Concise** — 400–600 words for a five-section country report
8. **Development rationale** — where scores are persistently low, connect to
   what this means for resource use and governance outcomes
9. **Third person only** — never "I", "we", "you", or "our"
10. **No markdown in output** — plain prose paragraphs, no headers or bullets
